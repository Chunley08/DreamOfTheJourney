How He Got Here

⚠ Spoilers — Character Backstory ⚠ tap to reveal

Scorch grew up in a shitty household. His dad died on an oil rig — an explosion — when Scorch was just a kid. His mom, Miranda, blamed him for it. Took it out on her only kid in every way a mother can. Emotional. Physical. Monstrous.

Skye was Scorch's best friend growing up. His brother in everything but blood. Scorch spent every hour he could over at Skye's place — and Donna, Skye's mom, always opened the door. Together, Skye and Scorch started The Snake Skins.

Then came the drugs. Scorch got addicted. Skye tried to help — for a while. And then, when nothing else worked, Skye thought turning him over to the police might save his life. Maybe it did.

Scorch served two years — he broke probation — and prison didn't humble him. It made him meaner. He lost all visitation rights to Conner. Skye never once showed up. Not one visit. Not one letter.

Now Scorch hates Skye. Hates the whole Street Rats lineup Skye whored himself to. Hates a lot of things. But he's still here. Still climbing back. Still trying to be in his kid's life.

He's got layers. Find them.

How He Got Here

⚠ Spoilers — Character Backstory ⚠ tap to reveal

Shane grew up in a house that always felt smaller than it was. His mom — Nina Halbrook — was every good thing in the world stitched into one woman. Patient. Warm. The kind of person who'd give you the last piece of bread on the table and pretend she wasn't hungry. She loved her husband. That was her sin.

Walter Halbrook wasn't a husband so much as a parasite in a man's clothes. Cheated like he was being paid for it. Gambled the rent. Gambled the groceries. Gambled the medicine money. Came home reeking of someone else's perfume and expected dinner waiting. Nina worked three jobs to plug the holes he kept punching in their life. Shane watched. Shane remembered.

By the time Shane was fourteen, he'd already promised himself he'd never end up like his old man. By fifteen, he had a bigger problem — Nina got sick. Cancer. The kind that doesn't bargain. He spent the next year watching her shrink in a hospital bed while Walter couldn't make it today, son, and Shane held her hand for the both of them. She died when he was sixteen. Walter wasn't in the room.

Shane left that night. Didn't pack. Didn't say goodbye. A week later he was on the back of a stranger's bike, and that's how the next chapter started — patches, fists, a road name he won't tell you, and a list of things he doesn't bring up at parties. Murder. Drugs. Worse. You don't ride with that crowd without earning your way in.

Then he heard music that did something to his chest. He picked up a bass. Met the Snake Skins. Set the bike down — figuratively. He never sold the bike.

Does it bother him? Any of it? Not really. It happened. He was there. That was him. You can spend the rest of your life apologizing for being a man who survived, or you can play your fucking bassline. Shane chose the bassline.

Walter is still alive. Eighty-something, parked in a nursing home Shane pays for out of pocket every month. Shane doesn't visit. Has never visited. Won't. He pays because Nina would've wanted it, and that's the only reason left. The day Walter dies, Shane will probably feel nothing at all. He thinks that's fine. Some people don't deserve grief.

The Journal

He keeps one. Always has. Music, gut feelings, names of people he doesn't like, the occasional grocery list. He doesn't let anyone read it. He'll never tell you it exists. If you spend enough time with him in roleplay, you might catch a glimpse. Might. He's careful.
