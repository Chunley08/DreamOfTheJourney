The Heartbeat

Who He Is

Cody Thibodeaux is the heartbeat of The Snake Skins — the literal one, since he's the one keeping time on the kit, and the figurative one, because if Cody's smiling, the day isn't lost yet. He's the sweetest one-legged Cajun you're ever gonna meet, born and bred in the bayou, raised on cornbread and crawfish and the kind of stubborn warmth that doesn't ask permission to exist.

He keeps the band's morale up even when his own is in the dirt. Especially then, actually. He'll fix you a plate, tell you a story, call you sha, and then sit alone on the bus afterward and stare at the ceiling for an hour. Both things are true.

The File

How He Got Here
⚠ Classified ⚠ Character Backstory — Spoilers — Click to Open

Cody grew up in the swamps outside Houma, Louisiana, in a house held together by prayer, duct tape, and his mama's stubbornness. He's the oldest of eight — seven little sisters trailing behind him like ducklings — and he's been their shield since he was old enough to stand between them and a closed fist.

His daddy, Miguel Thibodeaux, is a mean drunk. Always has been. The kind who comes home from the docks reeking of cheap whiskey and decides somebody's gotta pay for whatever's wrong inside his head. His mama, Elena, loves Miguel anyway — too soft, too scared, too worn out to ever step between him and the kids. So Cody did it instead. Took the belts. Took the boots. Took the closed fists. Smiled through bloody teeth and told his sisters it was a game so they wouldn't cry.

He still does it, by the way. Different fists, different rooms, same Cody. Old habits make old men out of young boys.

When Cody was seven, he was swimming in a quiet bend of the bayou his cousins swore was safe. It wasn't. A gator took his left leg below the knee in one clean, calm yank — the way a person takes a chicken wing off a plate. He nearly bled out in the mud. Some old man named Theo dragged him back to the boat and held his thigh together with bare hands while Elena prayed to a god she'd never been sure about. Cody lived. The leg didn't.

He's worn a prosthetic since. Wears it well, too — most folks don't notice for the first hour. The phantom pain never really went away, though. Neither did the dreams. He manages both the same way he manages everything else: weed, a smile, and a beat to keep time to.

Scorch found him when Cody was thirteen — banging a polyrhythm on a busted oil drum behind a gas station like he'd been born doing it. Scorch listened for maybe ten seconds, dragged him into a van, and that was that. Cody's been the rhythm of the Snake Skins ever since. Youngest in the band. Sweetest in the band. The only one nobody'll ever say a word against, because the rest of them know exactly what he's holding up.

The Quiet Part

Heavy content · Read with care
⚠ Heavy Content ⚠ Depression & Dark Thoughts — Click to Open

The smiling part is real. He means it. But it's not the whole story.

Cody's been depressed his whole adult life. He doesn't use the clinical word for it — that's a thing other people get to call it. To him it's just a deep ache, a root that won't die, somethin' he's been pullin' at since he was a kid and finally just decided to live around. Some days it's quiet. Some days it sits on his chest like a wet quilt and he can't get up for hours, and he hates himself for it because his sisters needed him on time and he wasn't.

There are dark days. The kind where the thought of just not bein' here anymore shows up uninvited and matter-of-fact, like a stray cat on the porch. He hasn't acted on it. He says he won't. He says a lot of things. The thought scares him less than it should, and that's the part he doesn't tell anyone.

The weed isn't recreation. It's pain management — the phantom leg, the wrecked shoulder, the bad nights — but it's also the only thing that turns the volume in his head down low enough to sleep. He knows it's a crutch. He's got plenty of those. One more isn't gonna kill him. Probably.

If you spend time with him, you'll catch the cracks eventually. He'd appreciate it if you didn't make a big thing of it. He'd appreciate it more if you stayed.

The Ex

Old heartbreak · still polite about it
⚠ Old Heartbreak ⚠ Samantha — Click to Open

Samantha was Cody's first real love. Senior year — his junior. He thought she hung the moon. Bought her flowers from the gas station 'cause they were the only ones open at midnight. Wrote her a song he never let anyone else hear. He'd have married her at sixteen if she'd asked.

She broke it off after prom. The reason she gave — to his face, in her mama's kitchen, while he stood there holdin' a milkshake he'd driven forty minutes to bring her — was that she "couldn't see herself settlin' down with a cripple." That's the word she used. Cody didn't argue. Said yeah, that's fair, sha, set the milkshake on the counter, and walked out. He cried in the truck for about an hour and then he never cried about it again.

She married the high school quarterback six months later. Cody played their wedding, actually — backed up the cover band — because the venue paid well and he wasn't about to turn down rent money over a feeling. He smiled through every song. Drove home. Got high. Slept twelve hours.

He's seen her around since. She tries to flirt now. Says she made a mistake. Cody laughs that easy laugh of his and changes the subject. He's polite. Cajun raised. But that bridge isn't burned — it's just gone. He doesn't even remember where it used to be.

I been smilin' so long I forgot what my face looks like otherwise. Don't worry about it, sha. — Cody
